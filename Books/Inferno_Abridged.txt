DANTE'S INFERNO — AN ABRIDGED RETELLING
Based on the Divine Comedy by Dante Alighieri (c. 1320)
Translated and condensed for modern readers


PROLOGUE: THE DARK WOOD

In the middle of the journey of our life, Dante Alighieri found himself lost. He had wandered from the straight path and could not find his way back. The year was 1300, and Dante was thirty-five years old — exactly halfway through the biblical allotment of three score and ten. Around him was nothing but a dark wood, tangled and savage, so grim that even the memory of it fills him with fear. He could not say clearly how he had entered it, for he had been overcome by a kind of spiritual sleep, a forgetting of the good he should have pursued.

As he wandered, Dante came to the foot of a great hill whose summit was crowned with light. He tried to climb toward it, but three beasts blocked his path: a leopard spotted and swift, a fierce and hungry lion, and a she-wolf so ravenous and gaunt that she seemed to carry the weight of all human craving in her hollow frame. Each beast drove him back, and the she-wolf, worst of all, pushed him toward the dark valley below.

Then, in that dim and desperate place, a figure appeared before him. It was the shade of Virgil, the great Roman poet, dead for more than a thousand years. Dante recognized him immediately — the master whose verses had consumed so many hours of his waking life. Virgil told Dante that he could not climb the hill directly; the she-wolf would always bar the way. Instead, Virgil had been sent by a blessed soul in Heaven — Dante's beloved Beatrice, who wept for him even in paradise — to guide Dante on a different road. They would descend together into Hell, pass through Purgatory, and ultimately reach the light. It would be a hard journey, Virgil warned, but it was the only way through.

Dante, trembling but willing, followed his guide into the darkness.


HELL'S GATE AND THE VESTIBULE

At the entrance to Hell stood a great gate, and carved above it were words that would freeze any living heart: "ABANDON ALL HOPE, YE WHO ENTER HERE." Through this gate passed all who had surrendered their chance at salvation. Virgil told Dante not to hesitate, and they crossed the threshold together.

Before they even reached the first true circle of Hell, they passed through a vestibule — a gray and miserable place neither inside Hell nor outside it. Here suffered the souls of those who had lived without taking any side, without committing themselves to either good or evil. They had been neither faithful nor treacherous, neither righteous nor sinful in any meaningful way. Because they had refused to choose, neither Heaven nor Hell would claim them. They ran ceaselessly after a blank banner that whirled through the air, stung and bitten by wasps and hornets, their blood and tears falling to the ground where worms fed upon them. Among them, Dante was told, was even one who had made the great refusal — some believe this to be a pope who abdicated, though Dante does not name him.

Beyond the vestibule, they reached the bank of the river Acheron, where the souls of the damned gathered to be ferried across by the ancient boatman Charon, whose eyes were like burning coals. Charon initially refused Dante passage, for Dante was still alive, but Virgil silenced him with a word from on high. The earth shuddered, the ground exhaled a red wind, and Dante fainted. He awoke on the other side.


CIRCLE ONE: LIMBO

The first circle of Hell was not a place of torment in the traditional sense, but of sorrow. This was Limbo, where dwelt the souls of the virtuous pagans and the unbaptized — men and women who had lived noble and blameless lives but had been born before Christ or had never received the sacrament of baptism. They had not sinned, but they lacked the faith that could open Heaven's gate. Their punishment was simply to live in longing, in a kind of perpetual incompleteness, knowing what they could not have.

Here Dante found a noble castle surrounded by seven walls and a bright stream, and within it gathered the greatest minds of the ancient world. Homer, the sovereign poet, stood at their head. Around him were Horace, Ovid, and Lucan. Aristotle sat among a company of philosophers. Dante saw Socrates, Plato, Euclid, Ptolemy, Hippocrates, Caesar, Aeneas, Hector, and many more. They moved in a meadow of fresh green, and their faces were calm, their eyes solemn. They spoke little, but their voices were measured and their bearing was one of quiet dignity. Dante lingered here with something close to wonder before Virgil drew him on.


CIRCLE TWO: THE CARNAL

Now they entered the true misery of Hell. At the entrance to the second circle crouched Minos, the judge of the dead — no longer the wise king of ancient myth but a monstrous figure who examined each soul and sentenced it by coiling his tail around his body the appropriate number of times to indicate which circle of Hell awaited. He warned Dante away, but again Virgil silenced him.

In the second circle were punished those who had been swept away by lust, who had surrendered reason to desire. A great dark storm raged here without ceasing, a hellish wind that flung the souls about in endless circles, dashing them together and spinning them apart, never allowing them rest. Dante saw Semiramis, Cleopatra, Helen of Troy, Achilles, Paris, and Tristan, and hundreds more.

Among them, he called out to two souls who flew together, close as doves. They were Francesca da Rimini and her lover Paolo. Francesca told Dante their story in some of the most heartbreaking lines in the poem. She had been married to Paolo's brother, but she and Paolo had fallen in love while reading together the story of Lancelot and Guinevere. "The book," she said, "and he who wrote it, played the part of Galeotto" — that is, the go-between. "That day we read no further." Paolo wept silently as she spoke. Dante was so overcome with pity that he fainted for the second time.


CIRCLE THREE: THE GLUTTONS

In the third circle, a cold and filthy rain fell without end — hail, and dark water, and snow — upon the souls of the gluttons, who lay sprawled in the mud like swine, wallowing in the foulness they had once craved. The great three-headed dog Cerberus stood over them, barking with all three throats, tearing at the shades with his claws. Virgil threw handfuls of the muddy earth into his three mouths to quiet him, and they passed.

Among the gluttons Dante met a shade who identified himself as Ciacco — a Florentine, a man who had been known in life for his indulgence. He spoke bitterly of Florence and its political factions, making a dark prophecy about the city's future strife. Then he sank back into the mud and said no more.


CIRCLE FOUR AND FIVE: GREED AND WRATH

In the fourth circle, overseen by the bloated demon Plutus, two great groups of souls toiled endlessly against each other. The hoarders — those who had clung to wealth with a miser's grip — and the spendthrifts — those who had squandered it recklessly — rolled enormous weights toward each other from opposite sides. When they crashed together in the middle, they hurled abuse at one another and then turned back to repeat the exercise forever. Virgil noted that many of them had been clergymen, cardinals, and popes whose obsession with money had damned them.

Crossing a bleak marsh, they came to the river Styx, black and foul, where the wrathful spent eternity fighting in the open swamp — beating and tearing at each other with hands, heads, feet, and teeth. Beneath the surface of the water, Virgil told Dante, lay the sullen, those who had nursed their anger silently in life; now they gurgled hymns in the dark mud below.

A ferryman named Phlegyas bore them across the Styx in a flat boat. As they crossed, a filthy shade rose from the water and grabbed at the side of the craft. It was Filippo Argenti, a man Dante had despised in life. Rather than showing pity, Dante expressed contempt for him, and Virgil praised his response. The other damned souls tore Argenti apart in the water.


CIRCLE SIX: THE HERETICS

On the far side of the Styx stood the City of Dis, the walled fortress of lower Hell. Fallen angels barred the gate and refused to let Dante enter. A heavenly messenger arrived, opened the gate with a touch of a wand, and departed without a word. Inside, stretching in all directions, was a vast cemetery of open flaming tombs. Here lay the heretics — those who had denied the immortality of the soul. The tombs were left open now; at the Last Judgment they would be sealed forever.

From one of the tombs a shade rose and challenged Dante: it was Farinata degli Uberti, a great Florentine nobleman and political enemy of Dante's family. Despite the flames, Farinata spoke with tremendous dignity, upright and unbroken. He and Dante argued about Florentine politics even there in Hell, and Farinata made a grim prophecy about Dante's own coming exile from Florence. Nearby, another shade rose — Cavalcante de' Cavalcanti, the father of Dante's closest friend — weeping and asking about his son. The exchange was one of the most humanly painful in the entire poem, and Dante carried its weight with him as he descended deeper.


CIRCLE SEVEN: VIOLENCE

A broken cliff marked the descent into the seventh circle, the realm of the violent. The Minotaur, the half-bull monster of Crete, raged on the rocks above them, and they slipped past him while he was occupied with his fury. The circle was divided into three rings for three kinds of violence: violence against others, violence against oneself, and violence against God and nature.

In the first ring flowed a river of boiling blood — the Phlegethon — in which the violent against their neighbors were immersed to varying depths depending on the gravity of their crimes. Centaurs patrolled the bank with bows, shooting any shade that rose above its appointed level. Alexander the Great was submerged to his eyebrows. Attila stood to his hairline. Smaller tyrants and murderers were submerged to their chests or waists. Dante and Virgil were escorted along the bank by the centaur Nessus.

In the second ring stood a dark and pathless forest with no green leaves — only black and gnarled branches bearing poisonous thorns. These were the souls of the suicides, transformed into trees, unable even to take human form in death since they had violently cast off their human bodies in life. The Harpies nested in these trees and fed on their leaves, causing a weeping pain with every bite. When a branch was broken it bled and spoke. Dante, in one of the poem's most harrowing passages, broke a twig and heard the voice of Pier della Vigna, a minister of Emperor Frederick II, begging him to be gentle.

The third ring was a burning desert of sand, upon which a rain of fire fell endlessly — like snowflakes, but of flame. Here were punished those violent against God: blasphemers, sodomites, and usurers. The blasphemers lay on the sand; the sodomites ran in circles; the usurers crouched with moneybags around their necks, their eyes fixed on them. On the burning sand Dante met the sodomites, and among them, to his sorrow, he recognized his own teacher and mentor, Brunetto Latini, who trotted beside him for a time and spoke with him with such affection and wisdom that Dante was moved nearly to tears. Brunetto even predicted Dante's future greatness before returning to his group.


THE DESCENT TO LOWER HELL: GERYON

To descend the cliff into the eighth circle, Dante and Virgil needed transportation. Virgil summoned Geryon, a creature of pure fraud — a monster with the face of an honest man, a body adorned with colorful knots and rings, and a scorpion's tail dripping with venom. This was the symbol of fraud itself: beautiful on the surface, venomous underneath. Dante rode on Geryon's back as the beast glided in slow spirals down into the abyss. It was one of the strangest and most vertiginous moments of the journey.


CIRCLE EIGHT: MALEBOLGE — THE FRAUDULENT

The eighth circle, called Malebolge (meaning "evil pouches" or "evil ditches"), was a vast funnel of stone divided into ten concentric ditches, each housing a different category of fraud. Dante and Virgil moved from ditch to ditch across narrow stone bridges.

In the first ditch, panderers and seducers marched in opposite columns, whipped by horned demons. In the second, flatterers were plunged in a ditch of human excrement, the filth they had produced in life now quite literally their environment. In the third, the simonists — those who had bought or sold sacred offices and sacraments — were packed headfirst into holes in the rock, their feet and legs sticking out above, with flames burning on the soles of their feet. Pope Nicholas III was among them, and he mistook Dante for his successor, Boniface VIII. Dante's response to the simonists was one of his most passionately political speeches in all of Hell, condemning the corruption of the Church without any softening of tone.

The fourth ditch held the diviners, astrologers, and sorcerers — those who had claimed to see forward in time. Their punishment was to have their heads twisted completely backward on their necks, so that they could only walk facing behind them, tears streaming down their backs and into the cleft of their buttocks. The image was horrible and grotesque, and Dante wept. Virgil rebuked him gently: pity here was impiety.

In the fifth ditch, grafters and corrupt politicians were plunged into a lake of boiling black pitch, maintained by a troop of demons called the Malebranche — the Evil Claws — who prodded them back under the surface with hooks whenever they rose. This section of the poem has a strange dark comedy to it; the demons quarreled among themselves, one farted a signal, and two of them fell into the pitch while chasing Dante and Virgil, who escaped by sliding down into the next ditch.

The sixth ditch held hypocrites, who walked slowly in a circle wearing beautiful gilded cloaks that were lined with lead — gorgeous on the outside, crushing on the inside. The seventh ditch was filled with thieves: here the punishments were grotesque metamorphoses as thieves and serpents interchanged forms with each other, since the thief had stolen identity itself. In the eighth ditch were fraudulent counselors — souls who had given wicked advice — each one enclosed in a tongue of flame. Dante spoke here with Ulysses, the great wanderer of antiquity, who recounted the final voyage he had taken after returning home from Troy — driving his ship past the edge of the known world, crying out to his men that they were born not to live as brutes but to pursue virtue and knowledge. His ship was swallowed by the sea within sight of a mountain he could not reach.

The ninth ditch held sowers of schism and religious discord, whose bodies were cleft and mutilated by a sword-bearing demon at every lap. Among them was Mohammed, split open from chin to groin. The tenth and final ditch held falsifiers of all kinds — alchemists, impersonators, counterfeiters — afflicted with terrible diseases, scabs, madness, dropsy, and plague.


CIRCLE NINE: TREACHERY

At the bottom of all Hell was a vast frozen lake called Cocytus, formed from the tears and rivers of Hell freezing in the absolute absence of warmth. This was the realm of traitors. The ice held them at varying depths depending on the nature of their betrayal.

In the first zone, Caïna, named for Cain who murdered his brother, were those who had betrayed their kin. In the second zone, Antenora, were those who had betrayed their country or political party. Here Dante encountered a shade frozen in the ice and stepped on its head; the shade cursed him bitterly, and Dante grabbed it by the scalp and threatened to pull out its hair unless it told him its name. The shade refused. This was Count Ugolino, and his story was one of the most terrible in the entire poem.

Ugolino told Dante that the head he was gnawing on belonged to Archbishop Ruggieri, who had betrayed him and locked him in a tower with his sons and grandsons to starve. Ugolino described waking one morning to hear his children crying out for food, and how he had bitten his own hands in grief, and how his children had offered him their own flesh to eat. When the last of his children died, hunger proved stronger than grief. The tale was told without melodrama, in plain and devastating words, and has haunted readers for seven centuries.

In the third zone, Ptolemea, were those who had betrayed their guests — a sin so grave that their souls were sent to Hell while their living bodies still walked the earth above, inhabited by demons. In the fourth and lowest zone, Giudecca, named for Judas Iscariot, were those who had betrayed their lords and benefactors. Here the traitors were entirely encased in ice, their bodies frozen in various contorted positions, some face up, some face down, some bent double.

At the very center of Hell, embedded in the ice to his mid-chest and towering over everything like a monstrous windmill, was Lucifer himself. He was not the beautiful fallen angel of other traditions, but a gigantic, grotesque, weeping creature with three faces — pale, red, and black — and three sets of bat-like wings whose beating generated the icy wind that froze all of Cocytus. In each of his three mouths he chewed a sinner: Judas Iscariot in the central mouth, his legs kicking outward; Brutus and Cassius in the other two — the betrayers of Julius Caesar.

This was the bottom of the universe, the point furthest from God, the center of all gravity and evil.


THE ASCENT

Virgil told Dante they must leave, for they had seen all that there was to see. He told Dante to hold on, and then he climbed down the vast body of Lucifer himself — down through the coarse, frozen fur — until they reached the pivot point at the center of the earth. Then, with great effort, Virgil turned himself upside down and climbed further, and what had been down became up. They were now climbing toward the other hemisphere of the world.

They made their way through a narrow passage, following a small stream that had carved a path through the rock over ages, until at last, after a long and difficult ascent through darkness, they saw through a round opening the stars of the southern sky. They emerged on the shore of the great ocean beneath an open sky, breathing air that Dante had not breathed since entering the gate of Hell.

"We came out to see once more the stars," Dante wrote — and with those words, the first canticle of the Divine Comedy ended. Behind them was the entire dark architecture of sin and punishment. Ahead lay the mountain of Purgatory, and beyond that, in a direction Dante could not yet imagine, the eternal light of Paradise and the face of Beatrice.

But that is another journey, and another poem.


— END OF THE INFERNO —


NOTE ON THIS ABRIDGEMENT: This retelling draws from the classic cantos of Dante's Inferno (Cantos I–XXXIV) and attempts to preserve the structure, atmosphere, and key encounters of the original, including the famous lines above the gate, Francesca's account of reading with Paolo, Farinata's unbroken dignity, Brunetto Latini's counsel, Ulysses' final speech, Ugolino's tower, and the vision of Lucifer at the center of creation. For the full experience, readers are encouraged to seek out a complete translation — those by Robert Hollander, Robert Pinsky, John Ciardi, or Allen Mandelbaum are among the most celebrated in English.
